The instrument

This is a small wooden tongue drum — six tongues cut from a single ¼″ top panel in a paired-slit pattern, mounted on a black-walnut box body. The original plan is from WOOD magazine, October 2008. I'm building three: the magazine baseline, an original bilateral-tongue version, and an extended-range variant — each phase of a key-tuning DoE study. Each tongue's pitch is set by length, thickness, and the wood's specific stiffness; the body's air cavity tunes the bass through Helmholtz coupling.

The same engineering instincts that make a stave-built djembe sound right are what make a medical-device prototype assemble cleanly.

Engineering note

Each tongue's fundamental is predicted by f₁ ≈ 0.162 · (h/L²) · √(E/ρ) and validated against a 43-column strike-level dataset borrowed from the tensile- testing protocol I use professionally. Phase 1 (this drum) establishes the noise floor and a species sweep across red oak, walnut, padauk, and maple.

Build method

  1. Mill ¼″ top panel from selected tonewood; verify thickness ±0.005″.
  2. Lay out paired-slit tongue pattern from the magazine plan, scaled to actual stock.
  3. Cut tongues on the CNC router; verify slit width and tongue length.
  4. Strike-test each tongue, record f₁; undercut as needed to drop pitch.
  5. Glue top to body, finish with tung oil, validate full tuning ±10 cents.
Played, struck, blown, bent, bowed, plucked.
